NURSING DIAGNOSIS: Altered nutrition: less than body requirements

related to:
  1. decreased oral intake associated with:
    1. anorexia and nausea (result from venous congestion in the gastrointestinal tract and can occur if digitalis levels exceed a therapeutic level)
    2. weakness, fatigue, dyspnea, and dislike of prescribed diet;
  2. elevated metabolic rate associated with the increased oxygen needs of the heart and the increased work of breathing;
  3. impaired absorption of nutrients associated with poor tissue perfusion.
Desired Outcome
The client will maintain an adequate nutritional status as evidenced by:
  1. dry weight within normal range for client (dry weight is achieved after fluid volume excess has been resolved)
  2. normal BUN and serum albumin, Hct, Hb, and lymphocyte levels
  3. improved strength and activity tolerance
  4. healthy oral mucous membrane.
Nursing Actions and Selected Purposes/Rationales
  1. Assess for and report signs and symptoms of malnutrition:
    1. dry weight significantly below client's usual weight or below normal for client's age, height, and body frame
    2. abnormal BUN and low serum albumin, Hct, Hb, and lymphocyte levels
    3. weakness and fatigue
    4. sore, inflamed oral mucous membrane
    5. pale conjunctiva.
  2. Monitor percentage of meals and snacks client consumes. Report a pattern of inadequate intake.
  3. Implement measures to maintain an adequate nutritional status:
    1. perform actions to improve oral intake:
      1. obtain a dietary consult if necessary to assist client in selecting foods/fluids that meet nutritional needs, are appealing, and adhere to personal and cultural preferences as well as the prescribed dietary modifications
      2. encourage a rest period before meals to minimize fatigue
      3. maintain a clean environment and a relaxed, pleasant atmosphere
      4. provide oral hygiene before meals (removes unpleasant tastes, which often improves the taste of foods/fluids)
      5. serve frequent, small meals rather than large ones if client is weak, fatigues easily, and/or has a poor appetite
      6. place client in a high Fowler's position for meals and provide supplemental oxygen therapy during meals if indicated to help relieve dyspnea
      7. instruct client to use herbs, spices, and salt substitutes if approved by physician or dietitian in order to make low-sodium diet more palatable
      8. allow adequate time for meals; reheat foods/fluids if necessary
      9. limit fluid intake with meals (unless the fluid has high nutritional value) to reduce early satiety and subsequent decreased food intake
      10. increase activity as allowed and tolerated (activity usually promotes a sense of well-being, which can improve appetite)
    2. perform actions to improve cardiac output (see Diagnosis 1, action b) in order to increase the absorption of nutrients and reduce venous congestion in the gastrointestinal tract (helps prevent nausea and reduce feeling of fullness)
    3. ensure that meals are well balanced and high in essential nutrients; offer dietary supplements if indicated
    4. administer vitamins and minerals if ordered.
  4. Perform a calorie count if ordered. Report information to dietitian and physician.
  5. Consult physician regarding an alternative method of providing nutrition (e.g. parenteral nutrition, tube feedings) if client does not consume enough food or fluids to meet nutritional needs.
